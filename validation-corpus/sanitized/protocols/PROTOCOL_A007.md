Study Duration: Approximately 12 months 
Study Phase: 1 
This study will be conducted in 2 parts. Part 1 is a single ascending dose study that will be conducted 
in a single center with healthy volunteers on a Phase 1 unit. Part 2 is a multiple ascending dose study 
that will be conducted in patients with mild to moderate persistent corneal epithelial defect (PCED)  at 
multiple sites in US, EU and AUS. 
OBJECTIVES:  
Part 1 
Primary: 
The primary objective of this study is to compare the safety and tolerability of placebo aqueous eye 
drops to a single drop of DUB-001 at ascending doses in healthy adult volunteers and to determine the 
recommended phase 2 dose (RP2D). 
Secondary: 
The secondary objective is to characterize the pharmacokinetic (PK) profile of DUB-001 in tears and 
plasma.  
Part 2 
Primary: 
The primary objective of this study is to compare the preliminary efficacy of placebo aqueous eye 
drops to multiple doses of the RP2D of DUB-001 administered at various frequencies. 
Secondary: 
The secondary objective is to characterize the safety, tolerability and pharmacokinetic (PK) profile of 
DUB-001 in plasma. 
Part 1 Number of Healthy Volunteers: Approximately 27 male and female volunteers (3 cohorts of 
9 volunteers each) will be enrolled in the study at 1 investigational site 
Part 2 Number of Patients: Approximately 36 male and female patients (3 cohorts of 12 patients 
each) will be enrolled in the study at up to 30 investigational sites

Study Methodology: This study is conducted in two parts. Part 1 is a single-center, double-masked, 
randomized, placebo-controlled, study with sequential enrollment to determine maximally tolerated 
dose (mtd) and recommended phase 2 dose (RP2D). Part 2 is multi-center, double-masked, 
randomized, placebo-controlled, sequentially enrolled study to determine the best dose frequency in 
patients with PCED. 
Part 1: 
Screening/Baseline: Healthy volunteers (HVs) will be randomized to the study after having understood 
and signed informed consent. Screening evaluations will be assessed over the next 14 days. Following 
the Screening Period eligible HVs will be admitted to the Phase 1 unit for baseline assessments. On 
Day 1 HVs will be randomized 2:1 to receive active treatment vs. placebo (DUB-001 vehicle). Nine 
adults will participate in each of 3 dose cohorts. 
Dose: Thereafter, on Day 1, clinic staff will administer a single eye drop in each eye from masked vials 
(either 1 active and 1 vehicle or 2 vehicle drops). Tears and sera from volunteers will be assessed for 
PK (a bioanalytic method will be developed). HVs will be observed for safety and tolerability for 24 
hours. 
Post-Dose Follow up: Baring any adverse events that require medical attention, volunteers will be 
dismissed from the clinic on Day 2.  They will be required to return on Days 7 and 14 for follow up 
assessments of safety, and tolerability. Volunteers will be monitored for adverse events (AEs) during 
the Post-Dose Follow up Period. HVs will be questioned about concomitant medications during clinic 
visits on Days 7 and 14. A telephone F/U visit will occur during days when the HVs are not in the 
clinic. Safety data will be collected thru Day 28 
Study Exit: The final F/U visit  and study exit will occur on Day 28 ± 3. Safety data will be reviewed 
by Data Safety Monitoring Committee (DSMC) which will decide if the next cohort can enter the trial 
and be dosed. 
Part 2:  
Screening: Patients of age 18 years of age or older with nonhealing neurotrophic keratitis (NK) of 2 
weeks or longer duration and consistent with NK Stage 1 (punctate epithelial keratitis) or NK Stage 2 
(persistent epithelial defect) corneas. Additionally, patients with herpetic NK without signs of active 
viral infection, NK secondary to Sjogren’s disease or NK secondary to diabetes may be entered into the 
trial. The NK must be persistent and therefore refractory to one or more conventional nonsurgical 
treatments. Patients with corneal ulceration/melting involving the posterior third of the stroma and/or 
perforation in either eye are excluded. 
Patients will be invited to participate in the study after having understood and signed informed consent. 
Screening evaluations will be assessed over 2 days.to differentiate stages and modify treatment and 
follow up.  
Washout: Symptoms will be confirmed on Day -5. On Day -5 to -1, patients will be asked to stop 
topical ophthalmic drugs that might confound the outcome of the effects of DUB-001.

Stopping Rules: Further administration of the study medication to a volunteer or patient within a 
cohort and dose escalation to the next dose level will be paused or terminated under the following 
circumstances: 
1. One volunteer in a cohort experiences a possibly drug-related SAE. 
2. Severe and possibly drug-related systemic or ocular AEs in ≥2 volunteers in a cohort 
regardless of system organ class. 
3. Sustained increase over 2 contiguous visits in IOP of >5 mm Hg from baseline for any 
1 volunteer and termination if in ≥2 volunteers in a cohort. 
4. Sustained increase over 2 contiguous visits in systemic blood pressure >159 mm Hg 
and or diastolic blood pressure >99 mm Hg for any 1 volunteer and termination if in ≥2 
volunteers in a cohort. 
 
Main Inclusion Criteria: 
Part 1 Healthy male or female volunteers with an age range of 18-64 years, both inclusive, who have 
normal ocular and physical health as determined, respectively, by an IOP <21 mm Hg, best-
corrected visual acuity (BCVA) 20/30, body weight 45-100 kg, body mass index (BMI) of 
17.5-35 kg/m2, and blood pressure no greater than 120/80 mmHg inclusive at the time of signing 
the Informed Consent Form (ICF). 
Part 2:Patients of age 18 years of age or older with nonhealing neurotrophic keratitis (NK) of 2 weeks 
or longer duration and consistent with NK Stage 1 (punctate epithelial keratitis) or NK Stage 2 
(persistent epithelial defect) corneas. Additionally, patients with herpetic NK without signs of 
active viral infection, NK secondary to Sjogren’s disease or NK secondary to diabetes may be 
entered into the trial.   
Study and Reference Drug:  
Study Drug: DUB-001: 1 mL preservative-free ophthalmic drops 
Reference Drug: (part 1) Vehicle, a matching preservative-free ophthalmic drop of phosphate buffered 
saline, pH 7.2 that does not contain DUB-001. 
Reference Drug: (part 2) Placebo, a preservative-free lubricating ophthalmic eye drop. 
 
 
Dose Administration:  
Part 1 
Clinic staff will administer a single drop of study medication [DUB-001 0.1, 0.3, or 0.4 µg/µL) or 
vehicle eye drop] into the eyes of HVs. One eye will be randomly selected to receive the active dose. 
The fellow eye will be dosed with DUB-001 vehicle  
Part 2 
Clinic staff will administer a single drop of study medication [DUB-001 RP2D) into the eligible eye.

Criteria for Evaluation: The sample sizes for this study were chosen based upon practical 
considerations.  No a priori statistical assumptions have been made.  Demographic data, including 
ocular history, will be summarized utilizing descriptive statistics. 
Safety Evaluations: 
Physical examinations, vital signs, and safety laboratory evaluations (ie, hematology, serum chemistry, 
and urinalysis) as well as a comprehensive ocular exam including an external eye exam, slit lamp 
microscopy, indirect ophthalmoscopy, assessments of visual acuity, visual field, pupillary function, 
ocular alignment and motility, measurement of IOP, and examination of the anterior and posterior 
chamber using spectral domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT).     
Pharmacokinetic Evaluations: 
The PK properties of DUB-001 will be determined through measurement of drug concentration in 
blood plasma and tear samples and determination of the maximum observed concentrations (Cmax), 
time to reach (Tmax), area under the concentration-time curve (AUC; AUC0-last, AUC from time 0 to 
the last sampling time point with a concentration difference from below the limit of quantification; 
AUCinf, AUC from time 0 to infinity), elimination half-life (T1/2), and apparent clearance (CL/F). 
Statistical Methodology:  
Safety Evaluations: 
Physical and ocular examinations, AEs, IOP, vital signs, and safety laboratory evaluations (ie, 
hematology, serum chemistry, and urinalysis) will be summarized using descriptive statistics.   
Pharmacokinetic Evaluations: 
Plasma samples will be analyzed to determine the concentration of DUB-001.  The following PK 
parameters will be evaluated for DUB-001: Cmax, Tmax, AUC0-last, AUCinf,  T1/2, and CL/F.   
Pharmacodynamic Evaluation: 
There are no pharmacodynamic measures in this study.

6. INTRODUCTION 
Pathophysiology  
Persistent corneal epithelial defects (PCED) are defined as corneal epithelial wounds that fail to 
heal at a normal rate following an initial corneal insult, despite conventional supportive 
treatments [1]. This condition arises following a disruption in the normal re-epithelialization 
process, leading to prolonged epithelial dysfunction and exposure of the underlying corneal 
stroma [2], [3], [4]. Disruptions of the integrity of the corneal epithelium activates a highly 
regulated cascade over 7-14 days that comprises proliferation and migration of epithelial cells, 
adherence to the underlying intact basement membrane. Any variation in this highly regulated 
process can result in abnormally slow healing. If the defect extends into the stroma, the 
proliferation of myofibroblasts will be activated with a resulting disordered extracellular matrix. 
Patients with a persistent corneal  epithelial defect (PCED) are at high risk for corneal melt, 
perforation, and secondary complications such as infection, stromal scarring, and vision loss [5].  
Evaluation 
Fluorescein staining is used to photograph the defect for serial monitoring and measuring of the 
size, depth, and location of the defect using a slit lamp. The condition may be accompanied by 
systemic comorbidities and relevant systemic investigations must be considered when treating a 
patient. Such comorbities can include diabetes, vitamin A deficiency, rheumatoid arthritis, 
Sjogren;s syndrome and other autoimmune conditions. Anterior segment optical coherence 
tomography (AS-OCT) can be used to quantify epithelial and stromal thickness and track 
progressive epithelial loss in areas of corneal thinning and scarring [12]. [13] 
Prevention of corneal melt, perforation, and secondary complications such as infection, stromal 
scarring, and vision loss requires prompt facilitated re-epithelialization. Clinically, the outcome 
depends on whether epithelial regrowth occurs before the stromal pathology progresses. Once 
the epithelium is intact, the risk of perforation is minimized. PCED can result from various 
etiologies, including ocular surface diseases, neurotrophic keratopathy, limbal stem cell 
deficiency (LSCD), post-surgical complications (e.g., after refractive surgery or corneal 
transplantation procedures), ocular injuries (including mechanical trauma and thermal or 
chemical burns).  Systemic conditions (such as diabetes mellitus), and long-term use of topical 
medications, including nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), chemotherapeutic 
agents, and preservatives in eye drops, can be risk factors for the development of PCED [6], [7], 
[8].

Unlike transient epithelial defects, which heal within 7 to 10 days, the primary therapy goal for 
PCED is to provide a proper environment for migration and proliferation of regenerating 
epithelial cells. Multiple factors influence the treatment regimen, therefore, a thorough ocular

Medical Management  
Most patients with moderate to severe PCED will have been managed with aggressive

bandage soft contact lenses are often used to aid re-epithelialization. Prosthetic replacement of 
the ocular surface environment (PROSE) devices incudes overnight lubrication with adjunctive 
use of antibiotics to promote re-epithelialization in some severe cases of PCED . Pressure 
patching or the application of decellularized, dehydrated pure amniotic membrane basement 
membrane (AMBM), a type of amniotic membrane tissue (AMT), was seen as successful and 
safe for healing persistent corneal epithelial defects in moderate to severe cases. 
Medical Need 
There is an unmet medical need in ophthalmology for a therapy that could heal the cornea with 
pharmacotherapy. Modern medical management does little to prevent subsequent corneal 
scarring, ulceration, opacification, corneal neovascularization, and, ultimately, visual 
compromise and vision loss after treatment. DUB-001 was developed as a simple eye drop 
composed of an oligonucleotide that pharmacologically dampens the metabolic cascade that 
leads to aberrant gene expression during corneal wounding.  
The active pharmaceutical ingredient in DUB-001 is a highly modified asymmetric siRNA of 20 
nucleotide (antisense strand) and 15 nucleotide (sense strand) that targets human ubiquitin 
specific peptidase 10 (USP10), a deubiquitinase. The chemical modifications to the 
oligonucleotides ensure stability while maintaining specificity for the USP10 target. A 
cholesterol moiety on the sense strand makes the molecule particularly efficient for promoting 
delivery into cells following topical administration [77]. The drug is formulated as an aqueous 
solution in PBS for topical instillation into the eye.  DUB-001 is being developed by DUB 
Therapeutics, Inc. for the treatment of moderate to severe PCED.

6.2.   Preclinical Study Findings 
The mechanism of action for DUB-001 involves regulation of aberrant expression of USP10 in 
wounded tissue.  The DUB-001 drug product interferes with fibrotic healing by employing an 
siRNA that directly targets USP10 mRNA and decreases USP10 protein expression. USP10 is 
798 amino acids (aa) in length and is expressed in the nucleus and cytoplasm of almost every 
cell. USP10 is evolutionarily conserved. Human USP10 has ~99% amino acid sequence 
homology with the rat and mouse proteins. The USP domain or catalytic domain (also called the 
core region) of USP10 is about 380-aa long and begins 415 aa from the N-terminus. The 20mer 
antisense strand of DUB-001 binds to the coding sequence of USP10 mRNA and downregulates 
its activity following wounding of the corneal, scleral, skin or liver.   
On a subcellular basis others have described that an increase in cell-surface expression of av-
containing integrins (avb1, avb3, avb5, avb6, and avb8) throughout many organs promotes 
fibrotic disease [78], [79], [80], whereas genetic silencing of av, and/or peptide inhibition of av, 
prevents fibrosis in mice [81], [82]. In fact, many integrin-targeting therapies are in the clinical 
development pipeline confirming interest in lowering av integrin levels and activity as a 
therapeutic target for fibrosis [83].    
Research in the Bernstein lab has demonstrated that after wounding, USP10 is upregulated in 
myofibroblasts. Removing ubiquitin from the intracellular domain of the integrin beta subunit, 
leads to the recycling of integrins to the cell surface instead of degradation, and their subsequent 
accumulation on the cell surface (Figure 1) [74]. Knockdown of USP10 gene expression results 
in increased ubiquitination and homeostatic degradation of integrins [74]. Modulating cell 
surface integrin levels by controlling intracellular degradation is novel.  In mouse and rabbit 
models we observed that application of a single dose has long-lasting pharmacological activity. 
This result positions DUB-001 eyedrops as a potentially effective method of control of scarring 
and inflammation during critical stages of wound healing. Other investigative modalities for 
treating PCED such as directly blocking integrins with antibodies and small molecules would 
require frequent dosing, incur difficulty with cell penetration, and further prevent wound closure 
[84], [85].    
Preclinical studies, summarized below, demonstrated that ocular treatment with DUB-001  
applied to the ocular surface of wounded eyes promoted corneal epithelial healing accompanied 
by reduced fibrosis and normalized neuronal ennervation. There was no detectable 
neovascularization in corneal tissue or effects on IOP.  A favorable safety and ocular tolerability 
profile will be demonstrated  for DUB-001 in future preclinical toxicology studies in advance of 
clinical trials.

8.1 Overall Study Design PART 1 
This is a single-center, double-masked, randomized, placebo-controlled, multiple ascending dose 
study in healthy volunteers consisting of a 14-day Screening Period (Day –28 to Day –14), a 
secondary screen for baseline values  Day –14 to Day –1)  to confirm compliance with all 
inclusion and exclusion criteria, a 1 day dosing regimen after admission to the phase 1 unit 
followed by a second day of close observation (day 2) , and 3 post dose follow up visits on Day 
7, 14, and 28. Study exit is on Day 28 (Figure 1).  Nine healthy volunteers will be randomized 
2:1 (active treatment vs. placebo) in each of 3 cohorts.  There will be at least 27 healthy 
volunteers in the study population.  Volunteers in the three cohorts will administered a single 
drop of DUB-001 into both eyes (see Section 8.3), on Day 1.  The 3 cohorts will be enrolled 
sequentially and identified as: Cohort 1 (0.1 µg/uL DUB-001), Cohort 2 (0.35µg/uL DUB-001), 
and Cohort 3 (0.45µg/uL DUB-001). A data safety committee (DSMC) will determine whether 
stopping rules have been breeched and dosing should be halted or amended between each cohort. 
The rationale for these doses is based on preclinical studies in which a dose of 0.35 ug/uL was 
seen as an effective dose in wounded rabbit models and human corneal explants. The dose was 
also considered safe in healthy pigs. Additional preclinical studies –see section 6.2.3 will be 
conducted prior to dosing human to determine whether these doses pose any toxicologic risk   
Volunteers will be randomized to active or placebo (DUB-001 vehicle) treatment during Visit 1.  
After randomization in the morning of Day 1 volunteers will submit to an initial PK blood draw 
before the first dose.  Volunteers will be confined to the study site for approximately 24 hours.  
During this time, the clinic staff  will monitor vitals, ophthalmic signs and symptoms of 
irritation, and AEs.  Additional PK blood draws will be performed after the first dose.  
Volunteers will return to the clinic during the Dosing and Evaluation Phases on Days 7 ± 2, 14 ± 
and 28 ± 2.

next cohort will be dosed after DSMC has evaluated overall safety, 28 days after the last dose of

worsens according to the treating physician’s discretion.

14.1 Definition of Adverse Events 
All AEs should be monitored and reported in a timely manner on the AE page of the eCRF 
during the study.  AEs will be captured from the time of administration of the investigational 
product through the follow-up period.  If AEs occur, the safety of the study volunteer should be 
the main concern. 
14.1.1 Adverse Event 
An AE is any untoward medical occurrence associated with the use of a drug in humans, whether 
or not considered drug related.  An AE (also referred to as an adverse experience) can be any 
unfavorable and unintended sign (eg, an abnormal laboratory finding), symptom, or disease 
temporally associated with the use of a drug, without any judgment about causality.  An AE can 
arise from any use of the drug (eg, off-label use, use in combination with another drug) and from 
any route of administration, formulation or dose, including an overdose.  An abnormal laboratory 
or ophthalmologic finding will only be considered an AE when the investigator determines the 
finding to be clinically significant.  Thus, any new sign, symptom, or disease, or clinically 
significant increase in the intensity of an existing sign, symptom or disease, should be considered 
an AE. 
The Investigator will ask an open question to avoid influencing the volunteer, eg, “Have you had 
any new symptoms, illness, or worsening of pre-existing conditions?”  Directed questioning and 
examination will be conducted thereafter when appropriate.  This applies to all AEs including 
SAEs. 
Notes: 
*An abnormality found during the Screening and the Predose Periods should be recorded as 
Medical History, but not as an AE. 
*Pregnancy is not to be considered as an AE but as an important medical event, which must be 
followed up as described in Section 15.2.4. 
14.1.2 Unexpected Adverse Event 
An AE is considered “unexpected” if it is not listed in the Investigator’s Brochure (IB) or is not 
listed at the specificity or severity that has been observed.  “Unexpected” refers to AEs that are 
mentioned in the IB as occurring with a class of drugs or as anticipated from the pharmacological 
properties of the drug, but are not specifically mentioned as occurring with the drug under

14.1.3 Serious Adverse Event  
An AE is “serious” if in the view of either the Investigator or Sponsor, it results in any of the 
following outcomes: 
• Death; 
• A life-threatening AE;* 
• Inpatient hospitalization or prolongation of existing hospitalization;** 
• A persistent or significant incapacity or substantial disruption of the ability to conduct 
normal life functions;  
• A congenital anomaly/birth defect; 
• Important medical event that may not result in death, be life-threatening, or require 
hospitalization, may be considered serious when, based upon appropriate medical 
judgment, they may jeopardize the volunteer and may require medical or surgical 
intervention to prevent one of the outcomes listed in this definition.*** 
Notes: 
*An AE is considered “life-threatening” if in the view of either the Investigator or Sponsor, its 
occurrence places the volunteer at immediate risk of death.  It does not include an AE that, had it 
occurred in a more severe form, might have caused death. 
**The following situations may not by themselves constitute sufficient grounds to be considered 
as an SAE: (1) hospitalization solely for a diagnostic purpose, even if related to an AE, (2) 
elective hospitalization for an intervention planned before the volunteer enrollment in the study, 
(3) admission to a day-care facility or sleep laboratory. 
***An example of an “important medical event” is a potential Hy’s Law case (eg, AST or ALT

21.10 Anterior Chamber Cells and Flare 
 
Grading of Anterior Chamber Cells and Flare:  
To quantify the extent of inflammation, cells and flare are recorded in the anterior chamber.  
Anterior chamber cells and flare are best seen by directing a 1 mm x 1 mm slit lamp beam into 
the anterior chamber using the highest illumination obliquely across the eye and focusing 
posterior to the cornea.  All measurements are to be recorded as normal or abnormal as shown in 
Table 16 below.  If an abnormal finding is discovered, the Investigator will determine whether 
the finding is clinically significant or nonsignificant.  Any clinically significant change after 
dosing will be recorded on the AE page of the eCRF.
