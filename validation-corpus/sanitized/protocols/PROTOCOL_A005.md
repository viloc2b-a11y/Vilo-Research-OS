The study will be conducted at three CLIA-waived Point-of-care (POC) locations

Study Objectives 
The objectives of this clinical study are as follows: 
Evaluate the performance of the [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] 1&2 [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] Test in fresh fingerstick 
whole blood samples from patients over 21 years old as compared with an FDA-
cleared comparator method.  
Evaluate [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] 1&2 [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] Test performance when the test is run by non-
laboratory, untrained users in POC locations representative of intended 
users/sites who perform testing under a CLIA certificate of waiver. Performance 
data are intended to support an application to the FDA for CLIA-waived status 
for the [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] 1&2 [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] test. 
Evaluate by use of a post-study questionnaire the ease of use of the simplified  
Quick reference instructions (QRI) and the robustness of the test in the hands of 
non-laboratory, untrained users in CLIA-waived POC locations.

BACKGROUND 
 
Genital herpes is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by herpes simplex viruses. Many people with 
herpes have no signs of infection and do not know they have it. An estimated 187 million people aged 15–49 
years experienced at least one episode of herpes simplex virus ([INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]) related genital ulcer disease in 2016 (1), 
and 491 million people aged 15–49 living with [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 worldwide (2). [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 is a sexually transmitted infection 
that is lifelong, incurable, and can cause recurrent genital ulcer disease and neonatal herpes. Vertically 
transmitted neonatal herpes is associated with severe morbidity (e.g., long term neurodevelopmental disability) 
and mortality (3–5). Genital [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 infection can also increase HIV acquisition and have a significant 
psychosocial impact on those with the infection. 
 
Genital herpes is the most common cause of genital ulceration in the developed world. More than 20000 cases 
of genital herpes are reported annually from sexually transmitted infection (STI) clinics in the UK and 500000 
new cases of herpes are estimated each year in the US (6).  Similar incidences have been documented in other 
developed countries. However, herpes is probably underdiagnosed, because many individuals do not present to 
health services. Most cases of genital herpes are caused by herpes simplex virus ([INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]) type 2. However, in 
some cities in Europe and North America, [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] type 1 is now responsible for more than half of new cases of 
genital herpes, particularly in women (6). 
 
With a seroprevalence of antibodies to [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 of 22% in the general population, genital herpes is 1 of the 3 most 
prevalent sexually transmitted infection in the United States (8). A central issue in the public health problem of 
genital herpes is the high proportion of genital [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] infections that are asymptomatic and unrecognized by both 
patients and clinicians. In addition, persons who are [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 seropositive may be symptomatic but nevertheless 
fail to recognize genital herpes and they serve as reservoirs for transmission. Physicians and patients must be 
aware of the asymptomatic shedding of genital herpes and the potential these patients have for transmitting 
[INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]. Recommendations to physicians for prevention include using type-specific tests for [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] when screening 
for other STIs and testing for [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] when evaluating patients with genital ulcers.  
The possible clinical use of type-specific serology is creating considerable controversy for STI 
physicians worldwide. Some authorities are now recommending the widespread use of type-specific [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] 
serology as a public health measure to control the epidemic based on the increase in [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 prevalence in the 
US and the observation that many patients attending a UK STI clinic would like to have the test (7). It has been 
suggested that an individual’s knowledge of their [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] status will lead to a more responsible attitude to safer sex 
and to a reduction in onward transmission (7).  
 
Testing for type-specific serology and antigen detection is recommended because of the different prognostic and 
counseling implications. Type-specific [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] antigen and serology will enhance the ability to make the diagnosis 
and guide clinical management in select patients. Researchers must evaluate the performance of type-specific 
tests and strategies to prevent transmission (8,9). 
Viro Research is responding to this global health problem by developing a rapid point-of-care [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]1&2 antibody 
[INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A] tests that can detect and differentiate antibodies to [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-1 and [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 infection. 
Currently, antibodies against [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-1 and -2 are detected by testing patients’ serum samples by either enzyme-
linked immunosorbent assays (ELISAs) or Immunoblot tests. Both of these types of tests require collection and 
processing blood samples to separate serum or plasma for further testing. In contrast, the Viro Research [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-
1 and [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A]-2 antibody will provide a single, simple and rapid point-of-care (POC) tests for the detection and

6.9.4 
CLIA waiver Near LoD/Reproducibility  
 
The objective of the study is to demonstrate that untrained operators in the intended use settings can perform 
the test consistently and accurately. The study design should include three external CLIA waived testing sites 
and at least nine untrained operators testing negative and positive samples over a minimum of five days.  
 
For this approach, reproducibility studies should be conducted only with untrained operators in CLIA WAIVED 
sites. 
 
This study will use one (1) verification/validation lot and follow guidance from the CLSI EP05-A3, EP-12 
(3rd Edition). To perform the study, the [INVESTIGATIONAL_PRODUCT_A], along with external controls, will be tested at three (3) separate 
CLIA waived sites in the U.S., by three (3) untrained operators per site, over five (5) days (these 5 days do not 
have to be consecutive). Along with the external controls, panels of venous whole blood (VWB) samples will be 
prepared, then blinded, and provided to sites for testing to determine precision of the assay in intended use 
environments. Each blinded panel should be designated to a unique user, as they will be designed to ensure 
that each operator should test a minimum of 5 positive samples and 5 negative samples during the study. The 
testing should be integrated into the daily workflow of the site where the operators are often multitasking between 
patient care, testing, and other duties.   
  
The sample panel will consist of a negative (expected to be negative 100% of the time), high negative (expected 
to be negative 95% of the time), low positive (expected to be positive 95% of the time) and moderate positive 
(expected to be positive 100% of the time).  A randomization protocol will be given to the sites so each operator 
knows which panel to test on each day, but no knowledge of expected results. The same sample panels will also 
be tested at the sponsor lab during the single-site precision study to confirm performance and to allow for results 
from the CLIA waived sites by untrained operators to be compared to the results of trained operators.   
 
Since the panels are in aliquot tubes, a sample should be pipetted in a plastic weighing dish to mimic the 
presentation of a droplet from a fingerstick.  The operator should then proceed with the testing as explained in 
the QRI.  Precautions should be taken to ensure that the plastic dish is properly labeled, matching the original 
tube the droplet came from to avoid errors in reporting the results.

10.2 Assessment of Severity 
Investigators will assess the severity of the adverse event and classify it according to the following definitions: 
 
Mild: Event/symptom is transient and well-tolerated by the subject. 
 
Moderate: Event/symptom causes discomfort and interferes with routine activities of the

causes inability to work. 
Note: These definitions are for descriptive purposes only and are independent of the judgment of whether an 
event is classified as an AE or an SAE. 
 
10.3 Assessment of Relationship to Use of the Investigational Device or Procedure 
The relationship between the occurrence of each adverse event and the use of the medical device (including the 
associated medical procedures) or control procedure shall be assessed and categorized according to the 
following definitions.  
 
During causality assessment, clinical judgment shall be used, and the Clinical Protocol shall be consulted, as all 
the foreseeable adverse events and the potential risks are listed and assessed there.  
 
 
DEFINITELY RELATED:  An AE that has a strong causal relationship. An AE that follows a strong 
temporal relationship, follows a known response pattern, and cannot reasonably be explained by 
known characteristics of the subject’s clinical state or other therapies. 
 
 
PROBABLY RELATED: An AE that potentially has a causal relationship. The AE has a reasonable 
temporal relationship and alternative etiology is less likely compared to the potential relationship 
to the use of the investigational device/procedure. 
 
 
POSSIBLY RELATED: An AE that potentially has a causal relationship. The AE has a reasonable 
temporal relationship to the use of the investigational device/procedure but alternative etiology is 
equally likely compared to the potential relationship to the use of the investigational 
device/procedure.  
 
 
NOT RELATED: An AE without any apparent causal relationship. The AE is due to the underlying 
disease state or is due to concomitant medication or therapy not related to the use of the 
investigational device/procedure. 
 
 
UNKNOWN RELATIONSHIP: If the AE cannot be determined to have a causal relationship, it will be 
classified as unknown. 
 
NOTE: Where the Investigator or Sponsor remains uncertain about how to classify the event, this should not 
cause them to exclude any possible relatedness, and the Investigator should consider classifying the event as 
“Possibly Related”.
